Intro

Hi. I'm Tae Bong Jeong, first-year graduate student in Department of Astronomy at UT Austin. My researches are mostly focused on computational astrophysics, which uses numerical methodologies to explain the Universe. Especially I am participating in research projects using cosmological hydrodynamic radiative simulations. I'm currently interested in first stars and first galaxies in the high-redshift Universe, mainly studying about their formation and evolution.

I used to work with Prof. Myoungwon Jeon from Khyung Hee University. I'm currently working with Prof. Volker Brom from UT Austin.

If you want to know about more information of my academic career, check my CV here.

Research

My research focuses on the formation and evolution of high-redshift galaxies using cosmological simulations. I am particularly interested in understanding how early galaxies form stars and produce strong UV luminosity in the first billion years of the Universe. Motivated by recent JWST observations that reveal a surprisingly large population of UV-bright galaxies at high redshift, my work addresses the emerging tension between these observations and theoretical predictions based on the standard ΛCDM cosmological model. By exploring variations in star formation physics—such as star formation efficiency and a top-heavy initial mass function—I show that early galaxies can appear UV-bright at fixed stellar mass without violating current cosmological constraints. My research highlights the importance of star formation processes in interpreting JWST results and in bridging the gap between theory and observations.

I also study the formation of Population III (Pop III) galaxies during the Epoch of Reionization (EoR). My current work focuses on a fundamental question: how massive can a Pop III starburst become, and can such systems be observed? Using cosmological simulations, I demonstrate that intense Pop III starbursts can form during the EoR, reaching total stellar masses of up to ~0.1% of that of the Milky Way. Combining these simulations with analytic estimates, my research shows that Pop III starburst galaxies could be detectable in current and upcoming JWST surveys.
